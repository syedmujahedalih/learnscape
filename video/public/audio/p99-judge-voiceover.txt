Most inference courses stop at diagrams. I built P99 to turn concepts into decisions.

A learner starts by following one request from queue to prefill, decode, and stream. Then they commit to a hypothesis before the explanation. Here, prefix caching should improve time to first token first.

In the playground, they change precision, batching, cache, concurrency, and decoding. P99 turns those choices into a reproducible experiment while holding the model, prompts, workload, and hardware constant.

The key boundary is honesty. No connected runtime means no benchmark. Attach local or cloud compute, and the same workflow can capture latency, throughput, queues, utilization, power, and memory from a real trace. Quality stays a separate evaluation.

I built P99 with Codex and GPT-5.6 during OpenAI Build Week. Codex helped me pressure-test the idea, pivot to inference education, implement the product, test the learning loop, document the limits, and deploy from desktop and phone.

Today, P99 ships the curriculum, prediction loop, experiment builder, and optional measured runner path. Next comes bring your own compute and, later, learned dynamics trained on diverse measured traces.
