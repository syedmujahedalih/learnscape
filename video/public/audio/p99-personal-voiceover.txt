Philip Kiely, author of Inference Engineering, writes: “Inference is the most valuable category in AI, but inference engineering is still in its infancy.”

That line captures why this space excites me. But when I tried to learn inference engineering, I couldn’t find a good interactive platform for building intuition. Most resources explain the stack. I wanted somewhere you could predict, tune, experiment, and understand what changed. So I built P99.

Start by following one request from queue to prefill, decode, and stream. Then commit to a hypothesis before seeing the explanation.

In the playground, change precision, batching, cache, concurrency, and decoding. P99 turns those choices into a reproducible experiment.

The key boundary is honesty. No runtime means no benchmark. Attach local or cloud compute and let a real trace report latency, throughput, queues, utilization, power, and memory. Quality stays separate.

I built P99 with Codex and GPT-5.6 during OpenAI Build Week. Codex helped me pivot, implement, test, document, and deploy from desktop and phone.

Today, the curriculum, prediction loop, experiment builder, and measured runner path work. Next is bring your own compute, then learned dynamics trained on diverse traces.

Learn the stack. Measure what actually happens.
