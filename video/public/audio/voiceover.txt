You start inside a launch-day incident. Traffic jumped six times. Requests are piling up, the GPU is saturated, and the service is missing its latency target.

Your job is to protect five things at the same time: latency, throughput, memory, quality, and cost.

First, make a prediction. Then change the serving stack. You can adjust weight precision, batching, the K V cache, concurrency, prefix caching, and speculative decoding.

Here is the important technical part.

P ninety nine uses a small learned dynamics model. It takes the current system state and predicts the next one: the queue, active requests, memory, utilization, throughput, and tail latency. Then it feeds that prediction back in and rolls forward again.

The baseline fails because incoming demand is higher than decode capacity. The queue keeps growing.

Now I switch to four-bit weights, batch requests together, reuse the shared prefix, and enable speculative decoding.

The learned model predicts that the queue will clear. Then a separate reference trace checks the answer. Latency drops below four seconds, throughput clears demand, and the incident scores one hundred out of one hundred.

So, is this a real world model? It is a real learned next-state surrogate. But today, it is trained on bootstrap traces from our reference simulator, not measurements from real GPUs. P ninety nine says that clearly.
