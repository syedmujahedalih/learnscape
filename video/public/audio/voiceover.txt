Inference engineering is usually taught through diagrams and disconnected benchmarks. I wanted P99 to make the learning loop concrete.

Start with the foundations. Each short lesson explains one serving concept, one controlled change, and the signals that would prove its effect.

Then build an experiment. Choose precision, batch size, KV cache, concurrency, prefix caching, and speculative decoding. P99 turns those choices into a reproducible specification.

Here is the important product decision. P99 does not invent performance numbers. If no runtime is connected, the result stays empty.

With the optional cloud runner attached, the learner commits to an outcome and runs the workload on a real GPU. The trace can report latency, throughput, queue depth, utilization, power, and memory. Model quality remains a separate evaluation.

The next step is bring your own environment. That could be local llama.cpp on a Mac or workstation, a university cluster, or cloud GPUs. A real learned world model comes later, trained and tested on diverse measured traces instead of bootstrap simulation.

I built P99 for OpenAI Build Week with Codex and GPT-5.6. Codex helped me pressure-test the original idea, pivot to inference education, implement the product, test it, document the limits, and deploy from both my desktop and phone.

P99 teaches the inference stack, then asks you to measure what actually happens.
